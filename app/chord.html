What is a chord diagram?
A chord diagram shows connections between groups arranged in a circle. Each arc on the outer ring is a category (a thematic family, a book, or a narrator). A ribbon connecting two arcs means those categories share something — the thicker the ribbon, the stronger the connection.

What does this tell you?
Family × Family reveals which Quranic themes co-occur in the same hadiths — e.g., how often prayer and purification appear together vs. trade and justice.
Book Distinctiveness shows what each book specialises in — only over-represented themes are shown, so you see what makes Bukhari different from Muslim or Abu Dawud.
Narrator × Book maps which narrators dominate which books, revealing the transmission network behind the hadith corpus.

What the overlaps reveal
Provision (rizq) connects most to Body (hands, feet), Travel, Worship, and Speech. The corpus is saying: provision is bound to what your hands do, where your feet go, how you worship, and what you say.

Prayer and Purification have one of the strongest overlaps — reflecting the prophetic principle that la taqbal salat bi-ghayri tuhur (no prayer is accepted without purification).

Knowledge connects heavily to Speech and Scripture — in the hadith corpus, knowledge is inseparable from its transmission through words and written revelation.

Paradise and Hellfire are tightly linked — the corpus rarely discusses one without the other, always presenting them as paired consequences.

What the distinctiveness reveals
Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah dominates in Fighting, Life & Death, and Wealth — consistent with its purpose as a comprehensive fiqh reference covering jihad rulings, inheritance law, and financial transactions.

Bukhari is distinctive in Knowledge, End Times, and Family Law — reflecting al-Bukhari’s editorial choices: he titled chapters to extract legal and theological rulings, not just collect narrations.

Malik’s Muwatta concentrates on Purification and Worship — the earliest surviving hadith compilation, focused on the lived practice (amal) of Madinah.

Books like Tirmidhi and Nasa’i show almost no distinctiveness — they cover all themes proportionally, acting as balanced encyclopedic collections rather than specialised works.

What the narrator network reveals
Abu Huraira appears in nearly every book with the thickest ribbons — the single most prolific narrator in Sunni hadith, with over 5,000 attributed narrations.

Ibn Umar and Aisha are the next most connected, but with different distributions: Ibn Umar is strongest in legal collections (Abu Dawud, Nasa’i), while Aisha dominates in Bukhari and Muslim — reflecting her unique access to the Prophet’s private life.

Books like Musannaf draw from a much wider pool of narrators (including lesser-known ones), while Malik’s Muwatta relies on a tight cluster centred on the Madinan transmission school.
