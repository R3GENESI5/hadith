The Story Behind Itqan

How reading the Quran word by word led to the first computational concordance

Bismillah: where it all began

I was four years old when my father held my hand and had me read my Bismillah. That was the beginning of a relationship with the Quran that would last a lifetime.

By the time I was nine, I was trying to understand what I was reading. The Urdu translations were more difficult than the Arabic itself, heavy, old-fashioned language that a child couldn't penetrate. I was frustrated. I wanted to know what Allah was saying to me, but the tools available felt like barriers rather than bridges.

But I kept going. The Quran was a friend through all those years. A companion.

2003–2010: when friendship became love

From 2003 to 2010, something shifted. I could not live without the Quran. Every day, whatever I could do: listening to it, trying to recite it, sitting with it even when I didn't understand every word. I didn't know Arabic. I didn't have a teacher. But I had the Book, and I had the desperate desire to understand it.

The way I learned was the only way I knew: I would read an ayah, then search the entire Quran for every other ayah that used the same word. Because Allah says:

وَنَزَّلْنَا عَلَيْكَ الْكِتَابَ تِبْيَانًا لِّكُلِّ شَيْءٍ
An-Nahl 16:89. "And We have sent down to you the Book as a clarification for all things"

Allah says the Quran explains itself, one part is explained by another. I experienced this firsthand. The word used in one surah carries a shade of meaning that only becomes clear when you see how the same root appears in another surah, in a different context. But finding those connections manually was exhausting. I would read an ayah with the word رِزْق (provision) and want to find every other ayah that talks about provision. Not just رزق but يَرْزُقُ، رَازِقِينَ، الرَّزَّاقُ. All the same root, all different forms. How do you find them without knowing every morphological pattern?

The book that changed everything

Then I found Fuad Abd al-Baqi's concordance, al-Mu'jam al-Mufahras li-Alfaz al-Quran al-Karim. A book where you look up any Arabic root and find every ayah containing it. It was exactly what I needed.

But you still had to know the root. If you didn't know that كَتَبَ, يَكْتُبُ, كِتَابٌ, مَكْتُوبٌ, and الكَاتِبِينَ all come from the same root ك-ت-ب, the book couldn't help you. And it covered only the Quran. What about the hadith? What if the Prophet ﷺ explained the same concept using the same root?

At the time, I didn't know Wensinck's hadith concordance existed. I didn't know anyone had ever tried to index hadith by root. All I knew was: Fuad Abd al-Baqi's book helped me find Quran words by root, and I wished someone had done the same thing for hadith.

2015–2017: my only companion

From 2015 to 2017, the Quran was my everything. My happiness, my sadness, my tears of joy, my embrace. I was memorizing it. I could not bear to be separated from it. Every surah I memorized became part of me.

During that time, the concordance was literally in my head. I could recall a root and find all the words from the Quran in my mind. Every root I encountered became a thread I wanted to pull, to see where it led across the Book. When you have memorized a surah and you encounter the same root in another surah, you feel the connection physically. You want to see ALL of them. You want to see the pattern Allah wove across 114 surahs.

Ramadan: where the idea was born

It started in Ramadan. I was deep in the Quran, using Fuad Abd al-Baqi's concordance to trace words across surahs. And I had discovered al-Furuq al-Lughawiyyah, the science of linguistic distinctions. Arabic doesn't have true synonyms. خَشْيَة and خَوْف both translate as "fear" in English, but they are not the same. رَحْمَة and رَأْفَة both translate as "mercy," but a scholar knows the difference. Every root carries a precise shade of meaning that no translation captures.

I was sitting with these books in Ramadan, tracing roots, reading the Furuq, and I thought: what if this could be an app? Starting from the Quran. Click any word, see its root, see al-Raghib al-Isfahani's classical definition, see the Furuq, how this word differs from its near-synonyms. And then, from that root, see every verse in the Quran and every hadith across every book that shares it.

I am not a developer or a software engineer. I didn't know what NLP was, or what a morphological analyzer does, or how to parse Arabic text computationally. But the idea was born in that Ramadan, and it wouldn't leave.

The first steps: Quran bil-Quran

It was the 19th of Ramadan, 2026, March 8th. The idea crystallized. And I started. Not knowing where it would go, not knowing how. Just starting.

The earliest work wasn't about hadith at all. It was pure Quran. Root databases, stem mappings, ayah-root connections. Then Mutashabihat ul-Quran, finding the similar and matching verses. The exact concept of "Quran explains Quran" turned into code.

Then it became Quran bil-Quran, a full app where you could click any word in any verse and see every other verse sharing its trilateral root. I grouped 1,651 roots into semantic families. I integrated al-Raghib al-Isfahani's Mufradat and the Furuq al-Lughawiyyah. The Quran was finally explaining itself digitally, the way I had been trying to do by hand for years.

It was deployed. It worked. But it covered only the Quran.

Then came the hadith

The question was obvious: what about the Sunnah? The Prophet ﷺ explained the Quran. His words used the same Arabic roots. If I could connect Quranic roots to hadith roots, I would have the first tool that bridges both corpora computationally, through shared morphology.

What followed was the most intense period of development. Every day a new exploration, every day a new problem solved, every day the corpus grew and the connections deepened.

And then I discovered Wensinck

It was only during development that I learned about Wensinck.s hadith concordance, a 7-volume work that a team of European orientalists spent from 1936 to 1969 compiling, doing for 9 hadith books what Fuad Abd al-Baqi did for the Quran. I had been building the same thing computationally without knowing it already existed in physical form.

But Wensinck's concordance and Fuad Abd al-Baqi's concordance existed in complete isolation. No one had ever connected them. The Quran concordance knew about Quran roots. The hadith concordance knew about hadith words. Neither bridged the two.

That bridge, connecting Quran roots to hadith roots, is what Itqan builds. And later, Wensinck's methodology would solve a problem I couldn't solve alone: 315 Quranic roots that my primary stemmer couldn't match to hadiths. Building an independent concordance using Wensinck's light-stemmer approach recovered 254 of them. Two methods, built independently, cross-validating each other.

Little by little, every day

I started. Little by little. One problem at a time. Every day was a new exploration. And every day, I am utterly astounded by Allah's mercy. How He helps me. How He lets me do it. Something appears in my inbox, a random video, a link, anything that comes my way, and it adds a layer into the project I didn't know was missing. This entire journey has been guided in ways I cannot explain rationally.

The Quran concordance
Root databases, stem mappings, ayah-root connections. Every word in the Quran traced to its trilateral root. 1,651 roots mapped across 6,236 ayahs. Integrated al-Raghib al-Isfahani's Mufradat and the Furuq al-Lughawiyyah. Grouped roots into semantic families: mercy, justice, prayer, provision, eschatology. Built Mutashabihat ul-Quran, matching verses that share roots. The Quran bil-Quran app went live.
The hadith bridge, and the first heartbreak
The Quran concordance worked. Now: what about the Sunnah? Downloaded 49,000 hadiths. Ran every Arabic word through CAMeL Tools. 32,413 words mapped to roots. Built the bridge: for each Quranic root, find every hadith sharing it. 384,016 connections appeared. I was ecstatic. Then I clicked a root and the hadiths were wrong. Completely unrelated hadiths were showing up. The bridge was broken.
The roots wouldn't transfer
This was the most frustrating period. The Quran uses one canonical form for a root, CAMeL Tools uses another. قضي in the Quran becomes قضو in CAMeL. بيع becomes بوع. أمن becomes ومن. 315 Quranic roots returned zero hadith matches. Not because the words didn't exist in the hadiths, but because the two systems disagreed on what to call the root. I built an alias map, 131 entries, painstakingly cross-referencing Quran forms to CAMeL forms. It recovered thousands of connections. But 315 roots still showed zero. I was stuck.
Ayah markers and shifting words
Then a different problem. The roots were not following the words. I would click a word and get the definition of the wrong word. At first it was off by one. Then by two. Then by ten. Then by fifty. Then by hundreds. By the time I reached the later surahs, the lag was thousands of words. Clicking a word in Surah Al-Nas would give me the root of a word from Surah Al-Baqarah. I had no clue what was happening. It turned out that every ayah marker, every delimiter, every verse number in the text was being counted as a "word" by the tokenizer. Each one pushed every subsequent root assignment further and further off. The lag accumulated across the entire Quran. The fix was invisible but fundamental: strip every non-word token before mapping roots to positions.
Defective roots and hollow verbs
Arabic has "defective" verbs where the third root letter is weak (و or ي). These change form unpredictably. هدي becomes هدو. صلي becomes صلو. The root extraction would work for strong verbs but fail silently on these. I would search for a root I knew existed in hadith and get nothing back. Completely dumbfounded. The fix required understanding classical Arabic morphological patterns that I had never formally studied.
No JSONs anywhere
Nothing was available as structured data. The classical texts existed only as PDFs, scanned books, DjVu files. I had to OCR them, extract text, write custom parsers for each format. The output was messy, full of artifacts, misread characters, broken diacritics. Every source required a different parser. Arnaut's Musnad Ahmad grades were in a DOCX file on Archive.org. Riyad al-Salihin grades were embedded in inline Arabic text on web pages. The 8 classical rijal texts were in OpenITI's mARkdown format, each with a different internal structure. Every single data source was a struggle to extract clean, usable data from.
The hadith IDs were lying
Then I discovered that idInBook restarts at 1 for every chapter in 14 of 18 books. Hadith #2 in Bukhari doesn't mean one hadith. It means 97 different hadiths, one in each chapter. Every Quran-to-Hadith filter had been showing false matches since the beginning. The entire bridge was producing wrong results. I had to rebuild the ID system from scratch with chapter-aware identifiers. After the fix, I verified every single connection: 100% accuracy.
Musnad Ahmad: the missing 95%
Ahmad had only 1,374 hadiths from sunnah.com. The full Musnad has 26,539. I was working with 5% of one of the most important hadith collections. Found the complete Arnaut edition on OpenITI. Parsed the entire Arabic text. The corpus jumped from 87,000 to 112,000. Ahmad became the 2nd largest book after Musannaf.
أبيه ,عمه ,أمي ,خالي ,جدي: the ghost narrators
I opened the isnad visualizer and there he was: "أبيه" (his father), showing as one of the top narrators in ALL 11 books. Hundreds of hadith chains flowing through him. But أبيه is not a person. It means "his father." Then I found أبي (my father), جده (his grandfather), عمه (his uncle), أمه (his mother), خاله (his maternal uncle), مولاه (his freed slave). All showing as ghost narrators. Each one needed a different solution.
The family tree inside the chain
"عن عمه واسع بن حبان" means "from his uncle, Wasii bin Habban." The uncle's name IS there, after the word عمه. But "عن أبيه" means "from his father" with no name given, because scholars knew that Hisham ibn Urwa's father was Urwa ibn al-Zubayr. I had to build genealogy tables: 37 father-son pairs, 8 grandfather pairs, 15 mother entries, 6 grandmother entries, 10 uncle entries. Each one researched from classical rijal sources. 76 entries total.
أبي صالح became أبي + صالح
Then the kunyas broke. Abu Salih (أبي صالح) is a single name, a kunya. But the parser was splitting it into two tokens: أبي (which triggered the "his father" lookup) and صالح (which became a separate narrator). Abu Huraira was appearing as two different narrators: أبي هريرة and أبي هريرة رضى الله عنه with the honorific. Every kunya in every isnad was potentially wrong. The fix required peek-ahead logic: if أبي stands alone, it's "my father." If it's followed by a name, merge them into a kunya.
22 classical texts, 111,604 profiles
Parsed 22 classical texts of ilm al-rijal from OpenITI: Tahdhib al-Kamal, Tahdhib al-Tahdhib, Taqrib al-Tahdhib, Mizan al-I'tidal, Al-Jarh wa al-Ta'dil, Al-Thiqat, Al-Kamil fi Du'afa, Tarikh Baghdad, Tabaqat Ibn Sa'd, Siyar A'lam al-Nubala, Al-Isaba fi Tamyiz al-Sahaba, Tarikh al-Islam, Lisan al-Mizan, Al-Durar al-Kamina, and Al-Kashif. 152,000+ entries of classical Arabic biographical text. Fuzzy-matched them against the existing database. 111,604 profiles with 178,859 name variants. 127,411 teacher-student links. The largest structured open-source narrator database available.
Wensinck and the breakthrough
315 Quranic roots still had zero hadith connections. I built an independent concordance using a light stemmer, and only then discovered it was the same method Wensinck had used in his 7-volume physical concordance from the 1930s. The light stemmer found hadith attestations for 254 of the 315 "missing" roots. 1,345 surface forms that CAMeL had never seen. Two completely independent methods, cross-validating each other. Root coverage jumped from 81% to 96.3%. The 61 remaining roots are confirmed by both methods to have no hadith attestation. The first empirical lower bound on the Quran's distinctive vocabulary.
Grading: who said this hadith is authentic?
112,000 hadiths and not a single one was graded. Bukhari and Muslim are self-authenticated as Sahih, so I tagged those. Then scraped Al-Albani's grades from sunnah.com for four books. Then found Arnaut's tahqiq edition of Musnad Ahmad as a DOCX file on Archive.org and parsed all 26,539 grades from it. Riyad al-Salihin grades were extracted from inline Arabic on web pages. 59,365 hadiths graded, 52% of the corpus. Every grade traceable to a named scholar: Al-Albani, Arnaut, or the compilers themselves.

The problems that made me laugh and cry

Every step forward revealed a new problem. Some of them were so absurd I had to laugh. Others made me want to give up.

The 42% that found a home
When I merged 21,465 entries from 3 new classical texts (Tabaqat, Siyar, Al-Isaba), 8,913 (42%) matched existing profiles. But the 58% that didn't match wasn't all new people. Many were the same narrators with slightly different spellings across texts, and I couldn't merge them safely. The same person, written three different ways in three different centuries, by three different scholars.
50 false-positive companion merges
I tried anchoring 8,243 single-source companions against 1,370 multi-source companions. Found 53 matches. Then checked them. 50 were FALSE. Multiple DIFFERENT people named "عبد الله بن الحارث" all matching the same anchor. The famous common-name problem of Arabic biographical literature. A problem that scholars have struggled with for a thousand years, now in my code.
Death years locked in prose
98% of new profiles lack death years in structured form. The years ARE there, written as "مات سنة ست وعشرين ومائة" (died in the year one hundred and twenty-six). My regex only catches numeric "سنة 126". Thousands of temporal anchors, locked behind Arabic word-form numbers. I built a word-form parser that handles 21 different patterns. It now extracts death years that are written out in full Arabic words.
The collision that wasn't
"أنس بن مالك" (Anas ibn Malik) exists as TWO separate profiles: ID 8 (the famous companion, 13+ name variants, 5 classical sources) and ID 17074 (a completely different person with mizan+jarh+tabaqat sources). Common names create phantom duplicates that look like real merges. You can't just match on name. You need death years, teachers, students, cities, and even then sometimes you can't be sure.
The ceiling
After three dedup strategies (companion anchoring, death-year clustering, teacher-student intersection), I found exactly 2 safe merges out of 77,794 profiles. The algorithmic approach hit its ceiling. The real solution isn't better code. It's better data: curated IDs from scholars who already solved this problem by hand.
Abu Huraira lost his عبد
The most prolific hadith narrator in Islamic history, Abu Huraira (عبد الرحمن بن صخر), was listed as "الرحمن بن صخر." The عبد had been silently stripped from 13,189 names (10.3% of all profiles). عبد الملك بن مروان became "الملك بن مروان." عبد الله بن عباس became "الله بن عباس." Every major companion and caliph had a broken name. I only discovered it when cross-referencing unknown profiles against graded ones and the names didn't match. The fix was a function that detects any name starting with a divine attribute (الله, الرحمن, الملك) and prepends عبد. 80+ divine attributes catalogued.
"بن ماجة" is not a father
1,388 profiles started with "بن ماجة." The parser was capturing the book source marker ("in Ibn Majah's collection") as the narrator's name. The text reads "ق بن ماجة أحمد بن إسماعيل" where ق is the sigla for Ibn Majah's Sunan. The parser ate "بن ماجة" along with the sigla, leaving either a broken name or nothing. The key insight: بن ماجة as a book name is ALWAYS preceded by a sigla. Real patronymics are ALWAYS followed by another name. This disambiguation rule fixed 11,761 entries with zero false positives.
The names that aren't names
Entire sentences posing as names: "قال مطين مات سنة ثمان وخمسين ومائتين" (Muteen said: he died in 258 AH) was listed as a narrator name. Complete hadith texts masquerading as names. The parsers were guessing where a name ends and a biography begins in a stream of Arabic text with no punctuation. I built a categorized verb system: citation verbs, editorial markers, isnad verbs, transmission verbs, biography verbs. A universal post-processing gate runs on every name regardless of what the parser did. Result: 31,000 names cleaned. Name quality went from 82.5% to 99.5%.
The Gawami al-Kalim heist
I needed an independent narrator database to validate our grades. Every website was a dead end: dorar.net blocks automation, islamweb.net is a book reader, sunnah.com needs an API key, Shamela has Cloudflare. Then I found it: inside a 2.8GB Windows desktop app from 2010, archived on archive.org. Gawami al-Kalim 4.5. Inside was Data/Rawy.zip (116 MB). The files were in a custom binary format, record separator 0x01 (SOH), tab-delimited, scattered control bytes. Cracked it open: 27,756 narrator profiles with pre-assigned grades. Cross-referenced: 2,578 new grades for unknowns. Validated: 91.4% agreement on 5,062 overlapping narrators.
The isnad chain that grades itself
40,000 hadiths from 6 canonical collections have explicit grades from Albani, Zubair Ali Zai, and Arnaut. Every graded hadith implicitly tells you something about its narrators. If a narrator appears in 191 sahih hadiths and only 12 da'if ones, they're demonstrably reliable. I extracted narrator names from 40,000 Arabic isnad chains, counted their appearances in sahih vs. da'if hadiths, and inferred grades. The danger: short names like "محمد بن أحمد" appear in chains of 358 different narrators. Safety rule: only match if 15+ characters AND matches 3 or fewer profiles. This gave 3,479 safe grades, each backed by statistical evidence from actual hadith chains.
100% traceability
5,593 profiles had no classical_sources field. They entered the database through AR-Sanad teacher/student chains or the Kaggle dataset, with no pointer back to a specific book entry. But they weren't phantoms. They all had teacher/student links, the chain IS the provenance. I built a provenance field for every profile: classical text entry (95%), Kaggle ID (1%), or teacher/student chain (4%). Result: 100.00% traceable. Every profile links to its origin. Not a single orphan.
Confidence scoring
Not all profiles are equal. An "أبو بشر" graded "reliable" could be any of multiple famous scholars with that kunya. The answer was the teacher/student graph. An Abu Bishr who teaches محمد بن جعفر and learns from سعيد بن جبير is a DIFFERENT person from an Abu Bishr who teaches يحيى بن أبي كثير. I built composite unique keys: name + death year + kunya + first teacher + first student. This resolved 7,511 previously ambiguous profiles. Now every profile has a confidence tier: A (ready for hadith grading), B (usable with caution), C (needs isnad context), D (unreliable identity), F (unusable). 96% of the database is uniquely identified.

What it became

112,221 hadiths across 18 books. 1,590 of 1,651 Quranic roots connected. 1,528,346 verified root links. 111,604 narrator profiles from 22 classical texts, 70.6% graded, 99.5% clean, 96% uniquely identified, 100% traceable. 160,614 name variants. 127,411 teacher-student links. 33,758 Arabic words with morphological definitions. 100,656 isnad chains parsed. A digital Wensinck concordance. Confidence scoring on every profile. 59,365 hadiths graded, 52% of the corpus.

What took Wensinck's team 33 years, this computes in seconds. What took a scholar physically opening four volumes of biographical dictionaries, this consolidates into one search. What no one had ever done, connecting the Quran concordance to the hadith concordance through shared roots, is now a click away.

And it is free. And it will remain free. Because this is a sadaqah jariyah.

مَن سَنَّ فِي الإِسْلَامِ سُنَّةً حَسَنَةً فَلَهُ أَجْرُهَا وَأَجْرُ مَنْ عَمِلَ بِهَا

What comes next

The narrator's portrait

Every narrator has a story. The hadiths they narrate are the hadiths they carried through their lives, what they heard, what they memorized, what they chose to transmit. A narrator's collection IS their portrait: Abu Huraira's 5,000+ narrations paint a picture of a man who spent every moment near the Prophet. Aisha's narrations reveal the private life no one else had access to. Ali's cluster in Ahmad reflects the Kufan transmission school. The next step is to build this portrait: for every narrator, show the actual hadiths they carried, the themes they transmitted, the scholars they taught.

The dedup problem

111,604 profiles from 22 classical texts. But thousands are duplicates across texts, the same narrator listed differently in Tahdhib al-Kamal and Mizan al-I'tidal and Tarikh al-Islam. The algorithmic approach hit its ceiling at 6,394 merges. The next step needs curated scholarly IDs from projects like dorar.net, n007rehan, and Mawsu'at al-Hadith, scholars who already solved this problem by hand over decades.

The remaining 48%

52% of hadiths are graded. The remaining 48%: Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah (38,000), Muwatta Malik, Darimi, and 7 smaller books await grading sources. The 22 classical texts have grades embedded in narrative prose that NLP could extract. And the 61 Quran-only roots (confirmed by dual-stemmer validation) await deeper linguistic analysis.
